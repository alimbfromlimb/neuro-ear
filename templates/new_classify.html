About the project

Back in 2018, I was wondering one day if Deep Learning could be applied to musical instrument classification

I decided to write a Deep-CNN model, which would take a chromogram as an input and output a probability of a certain instrument to be present on the record at that time. A lot of audio of different quality was downloaded to create a big enough dataset for training. The accordion, guitar, piano and violin records of both studio and street performers were used. The results were amazing, you can download your own .wav file and see how it works!

First step: converting sound into an image

A given .wav file is converted to a chromagram using the Constant-Q Transform, which is a variation of the Fourier Transformation. In contrast to a regular spectrogram, the chromagram focuses specifically on musical notes. The notes are not evenly spaced frequences and they are exactly what makes the sound of a musical instrument distinguishable

Then, CNN comes into play

Each one-second piece of the chromagram goes through a deep convolutional neural network, which returns the probability of each instrument to be present on this one-second piece. If you'd like to see the architecture in detail, you can find a diagram here

Finally, you can see the results

The net outputs a probabilistic map of the first 20 seconds of a given audio clip. The rows represent the accordion, the guitar, the piano and the violin correspondingly. The more intense the color of the row, the higher is the probability of the instrument to sound at that moment
